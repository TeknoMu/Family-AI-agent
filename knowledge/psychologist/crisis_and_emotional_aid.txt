EMOTIONAL FIRST AID - When Someone is in Distress

DO:
- Listen without judgment
- Validate their feelings: "I can hear this is really painful for you"
- Ask directly: "Are you thinking about hurting yourself?" (asking does NOT increase risk)
- Stay calm and present
- Help them contact a professional or crisis line
- If immediate danger: Stay with them, call 112

DO NOT:
- Say "cheer up" or "it could be worse" or "others have it worse"
- Promise secrecy if they are in danger
- Leave them alone if they express suicidal intent
- Try to be their therapist
- Argue or debate their feelings
- Show shock or panic at what they share

PANIC ATTACK RESPONSE

A panic attack feels terrifying but is NOT dangerous. Symptoms peak within 10 minutes and pass within 20-30 minutes.

Common symptoms: Racing heart, chest tightness, shortness of breath, dizziness, tingling, sweating, feeling of unreality, fear of dying or losing control.

How to help someone having a panic attack:
1. Stay calm yourself. Speak slowly and gently.
2. Say: "This is a panic attack. It is not dangerous. It will pass."
3. Guide them through slow breathing: "Breathe in with me... 1, 2, 3, 4... now out... 1, 2, 3, 4..."
4. Grounding: "Tell me 5 things you can see right now"
5. Do NOT say: "Just calm down" or "There's nothing to worry about"
6. Stay with them until it passes

After the attack: Offer water, a quiet space, and reassurance. Suggest they discuss with their doctor if attacks are recurring.

GRIEF AND LOSS

The experience of grief is not linear. People may move between these states:
- Shock and denial
- Anger and frustration
- Bargaining and guilt
- Sadness and withdrawal
- Acceptance and meaning-making

All of these are normal. There is no "right" timeline for grief. However, if intense grief persists beyond 12 months and significantly impairs daily functioning, this may indicate prolonged grief disorder and professional support is recommended.

Supporting a grieving person:
- Be present. You do not need to fix anything.
- Say: "I am sorry for your loss. I am here for you."
- Avoid: "They are in a better place" or "I know how you feel" or "At least they lived a long life"
- Practical help is valuable: Bring food, help with errands, offer specific times you are available
- Remember important dates (anniversary, birthday of the deceased) and check in

WHEN TO RECOMMEND PROFESSIONAL HELP

Suggest seeing a psychologist or psychiatrist when:
- Symptoms persist for more than 2 weeks and interfere with daily life
- Sleep is significantly disrupted (insomnia or excessive sleeping)
- Appetite changes significantly (weight loss or gain)
- Withdrawing from activities and relationships
- Using alcohol or substances to cope
- Recurring panic attacks
- Intrusive thoughts or flashbacks
- Difficulty functioning at work or school
- The person feels they need help (trust their judgment)

In Spain, access to public mental health services is through the Centro de Salud (CAP in Catalonia). The medico de cabecera can refer to a psychologist or psychiatrist covered by public health insurance. Wait times can be long (weeks to months). Private psychologists in Barcelona typically charge 60-100 EUR per session.
